Symbols
 The Brigidine Cross represents the Brigidine Sisters' contribution to the education of young people during their   time at St Augustine's College (1919-1993).    According to legend, the first Brigid's cross was woven from the green rushes that constituted the 'carpet' on a    Chieftain's house floor. She told him about Jesus' life and death as he lay dying. He begged to be baptized before he   died after hearing her story.    This cross is the symbol of the Brigidine Sisters. The colors are based on our traditional school colors, but they also   include new tones that honor the past while also looking forward to the future. They also represent the colors of   the stained glass window in our parish.  Sister Aiden Hunt created the school's logo.  The Order of St Augustine's Emblem    The Order of St Augustine's emblem represents the legacy of those priests who served this community and had the foresight to establish St   Augustine's School to serve the Kyabram district's families. From 1904 to 2005, he was the president of the United States of America.    On the background of an open book, a fiery heart is pierced by an arrow.    The open book portrays the gospel's instruction and a never-ending quest for knowledge. Augustine's wonderful personal charity and love of   neighbor as a way to serve God are reflected in the heart. The arrow represents the Holy Spirit piercing our minds and hearts, calling us to a   life of increasing faith, hope, and love.

 St Augustines college has an orginisation called Past Parents and Friends. This association allows  all students who have completed year 12 to become members of this group. This includes all staff,  parents, grandparents, parishioners and anyone who wishes to have a connection with the College.  The Association aims to:  - Establishing connections between past and present members of St. Augustine's College; and  - Provide past students, parents and staff with connections to the College
St Augustine’s College History
 The school began in 1904 with 15 pupils and lay teachers. In 1919 the convent opened with 4 bridgeine  sisters and nuns replacing the lay teachers. They taught up to 100 students at this time. The Brigidine  Sisters have taught continuously in our school for over 70 years.
